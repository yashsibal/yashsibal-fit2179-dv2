How Australia Moves

Australia is still a sporting country, but the clearest adult participation story is now bigger than club sport. Walking, fitness/gym and other flexible activities dominate how Australians stay active.

12 Vega-Lite diagrams 1 geographic map 2 official data sources
Main question

Are Australians mainly moving through traditional organised sport, or through flexible activities that fit around everyday life?

50.2%of adults participated in recreational walking
34.1%participated in fitness or gym activities
85.9%participated in sport or physical activity at least once a year

Primary data: Australian Sports Commission AusPlay Data Tables, January–December 2025. Population context: ABS National, state and territory population, September 2025.

What

Adult sport, fitness and physical activity participation, combined with state and territory population context.

Why

To show that Australian participation is broader than club sport, competition and organised teams.

Who

Average Australian viewers, using clear non-technical language and direct chart annotations.

How

Rankings, stacked bars, heatmaps, a projected map, scatterplot, diverging bars, small multiples and a custom spectrum.

Reading guide

Every chart answers one part of the same story.

This page moves from the national picture to activity type, geography and demographics. Participation rates are not mutually exclusive: one person can appear in several activities, so the goal is comparison, not adding percentages into a total.

01 / Activity snapshot

The biggest activities are not the classic club sports.

Australia is often described through competitive sport culture. AusPlay shows a more everyday pattern: walking, fitness/gym, bush walking, running and swimming all sit above the major traditional team sports in adult participation.

02 / Fitness shift

Flexible fitness is the everyday option.

The most accessible activities are easy to do alone, locally and outside a fixed club schedule. This makes them more compatible with work, study, cost and time pressure.

03 / Where Australia moves

Participation is high everywhere, but geography still matters.

The ACT records the highest rate in this comparison. Larger states such as NSW and Victoria have far more participants overall, but population size does not automatically produce a higher rate.

04 / Who participates?

Participation patterns change by gender and life stage.

The national ranking hides meaningful differences between groups. Splitting by gender and age reveals where activities are balanced and where participation is concentrated.

Visualisation 10

Male and female participation

Diverging bars · group comparison

Key read: Walking is higher among females in this dataset, while running/jogging is higher among males.

Design choice: A diverging layout makes the direction and size of the gender difference visible without extra calculation.

Visualisation 11

05 / Final takeaway

Australia is sporty, but it is also increasingly flexible.

The strongest adult participation story is not only about organised teams and competition. It is also about activities people can do alone, casually, locally and on their own schedule.

Visualisation 12

Sport-to-fitness spectrum

Custom idiom · derived placement

Key read: The largest circles are on the flexible fitness side, especially walking and fitness/gym.

Design choice: Position is a derived storytelling classification, while circle size uses the real AusPlay adult participation rate.

Final message

The modern Australian participation story is not sport versus fitness. It is sport plus fitness.

Traditional sports still matter culturally and socially, but the participation data shows that everyday, flexible and individual activities are now central to how Australian adults stay active.
