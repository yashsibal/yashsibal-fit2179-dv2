How Australia Moves

Australia is often described as a sporting nation. AusPlay data shows a wider story: adults are staying active through flexible, everyday activities as much as organised sport.

50.2% of adults participated in recreational walking
34.1% participated in fitness or gym activities
85.9% participated in sport or physical activity at least once a year

Primary data: Australian Sports Commission AusPlay Data Tables, January–December 2025. Population context: ABS National, state and territory population, September 2025.

01

Australia’s activity snapshot

Traditional sport is still visible, but the biggest adult participation activities are everyday options. Walking and fitness/gym sit above every traditional club or team sport in the dataset.

Adult participation rates are not mutually exclusive: one person can appear in multiple activities.

The official AusPlay categories show whether active adults participated in sport-related activity, non-sport activity, or both.

Older age groups lean more toward non-sport activity, while younger groups retain a stronger sport component.

02

Flexible fitness is the everyday option

Flexible activities are easier to fit around work, study, cost, weather and time. Walking, gym, running, swimming and cycling form the centre of the participation story.

These activities usually do not require a fixed team, club season or match schedule.

Traditional sports still matter, but they sit below walking and fitness/gym in adult participation.

The heatmap makes the life-stage pattern clearer: some activities peak in youth, while walking remains high into older age groups.

03

Where Australia moves

Participation is high across every state and territory. A projected symbol map shows the geographic pattern, while the ranking chart gives the exact comparison.

Projected symbol map using approximate state and territory capital positions for placement. Circle size shows estimated adult participants.

The ACT records the highest adult participation rate in this AusPlay comparison; Queensland is the lowest.

This combines the two required data sources: AusPlay participation rates and ABS state/territory population data.

04

Who participates?

The overall story changes when activity choices are split by gender and age. Some activities are balanced, while others show clear differences in participation patterns.

The diverging layout places female participation to the left and male participation to the right so differences are easier to compare.

Small multiples avoid squeezing every activity into one crowded line chart.

05

Final takeaway

Australia’s identity as a sporting nation still makes sense, but it is incomplete. The strongest participation story in 2025 is also about activities people can do alone, casually, locally and on their own schedule.

The spectrum is a custom storytelling visualisation. Horizontal position is a derived classification of flexibility, while circle size uses real AusPlay participation rates.
