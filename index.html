How Australia Moves

Australia is still a sporting country, but AusPlay participation data shows a wider story: everyday fitness and flexible lifestyle activities are now central to how adults stay active.

12 Vega-Lite visualisations 2 official data sources 1 geographic map
Main question

Are Australians mainly participating through traditional organised sport, or through flexible activities like walking, gym and running?

50.2%of adults participated in recreational walking
34.1%participated in fitness or gym activities
85.9%participated in sport or physical activity at least once a year

Primary data: Australian Sports Commission AusPlay Data Tables, January–December 2025. Population context: ABS National, state and territory population, September 2025.

What

Adult sport, fitness and physical activity participation, combined with state and territory population context.

Why

To show that the way Australians stay active is broader than club sport and competition.

Who

Average Australian viewers who want a clear, non-technical view of participation patterns.

How

Rankings, stacked bars, heatmaps, a map, scatterplot, diverging bars, small multiples and a custom spectrum.

01 / Activity snapshot

The biggest activities are not the classic club sports.

Australia is often described through competitive sport culture. The participation data shows something more everyday: walking, fitness/gym and other flexible activities sit above traditional sports in adult participation.

Visualisation 1Top activities

Activity rates are not mutually exclusive. One adult can appear in multiple activities.

Visualisation 2Sport vs non-sport

AusPlay separates adults by whether their participation was sport-related, non-sport-related, or both.

Visualisation 3Age balance

The balance shifts with age: older adults lean much more strongly toward non-sport activity.

02 / Fitness shift

Flexible fitness is the everyday option.

Activities such as walking, gym, running and swimming are easier to fit around work, study, cost and time. This makes them central to modern participation.

Visualisation 4Flexible activities

These activities usually do not require a fixed club season, team selection or match schedule.

Visualisation 5Traditional sports

Traditional sports remain meaningful, but their adult participation rates sit below the biggest fitness activities.

Visualisation 6Age by activity heatmap

The heatmap makes the life-stage pattern visible: some activities peak among younger adults, while walking remains high later in life.

03 / Where Australia moves

Participation is high everywhere, but geography still matters.

The ACT records the highest adult participation rate in this comparison. Larger states contribute far more participants overall, but population size does not automatically mean a higher participation rate.

Visualisation 7Projected symbol map

The map uses a simplified Australia outline for geographic context. Symbols represent state/territory participation values.

Visualisation 8State ranking

The ranking view supports the map by making exact state and territory comparisons easier.

Visualisation 9Population context

This chart combines the two required data sources: AusPlay participation rates and ABS state/territory population data.

04 / Who participates?

Participation patterns change by gender and life stage.

The overall story becomes more interesting when split by activity. Some choices are relatively balanced, while others show clear gender or age patterns.

Visualisation 10Gender diverging bars

Female participation is shown to the left and male participation to the right so the gap can be compared quickly.

Visualisation 11Small multiples by age

Small multiples avoid forcing every activity into one crowded line chart.

05 / Final takeaway

Australia is sporty, but it is also increasingly flexible.

The strongest participation story is not only about organised teams and competition. It is also about activities people can do alone, casually, locally and on their own schedule.
